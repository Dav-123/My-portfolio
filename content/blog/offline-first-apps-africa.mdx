# Why Every African App Should Be Offline-First

Most developers build apps that assume a stable internet connection. In Nigeria, that assumption will break your product.

## The Reality of Connectivity in Nigeria

Average mobile internet speed in Nigeria hovers around 15–20 Mbps — on a good day. In university hostels, markets, and rural areas, you're lucky to get 2G. Power cuts mean recharging hotspots is unpredictable.

Your app needs to work *despite* this, not *because of* ideal conditions.

## What Offline-First Actually Means

Offline-first doesn't mean your app works without internet forever. It means:

1. **Core functionality works without a connection**
2. **Data syncs intelligently when connectivity returns**
3. **The user never sees a blank screen or error just because of poor signal**

## Tools I Use

For web apps, IndexedDB with a service worker strategy is powerful. For React Native, SQLite via `expo-sqlite` or `@op-engineering/op-sqlite` handles local persistence beautifully. For sync, a queue-based approach — store actions locally, replay them when online — works reliably.

## B-Chat as a Case Study

B-Chat, my Bluetooth mesh messaging app, takes this further: it eliminates internet dependency entirely. Messages hop between phones via Bluetooth. No towers. No data. Just physics.

## The Bottom Line

If your app dies when connectivity drops, you're building for the wrong Africa. Build for the real one.
